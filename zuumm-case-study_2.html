01 · The Problem

Travel planning is broken —
and everyone just accepted it.

The way most travel platforms work hasn't fundamentally changed in 20 years. Open MakeMyTrip, Booking.com, Google Flights — and the first thing they ask is: "Where do you want to go?"

But that's not how people actually think. They think: "somewhere cold and quiet for 4 days" or "a beach trip — kid-friendly, under ₹50K." They don't start with a destination. They start with a feeling, a budget, a mood — a vibe.

"The core UX failure of every travel platform is asking users for an answer before they've figured out the question."

Zuumm.ai evolved from IncBuddy — a corporate travel platform serving 100+ enterprise clients. When they pivoted to consumer and B2B holiday packages, they needed a fundamentally different experience: helping people who don't know what they want yet.

My job was to design around a single insight — vibe-based search, not destination-based search — and make it work at product scale.

Business Challenge

Differentiate in a saturated OTA market. Convert a corporate travel brand into a consumer-trusted product with AI as the core differentiator.

UX Challenge

Design a search system natural to users who have no destination in mind — and surface curated packages that match abstract, emotional intent.

02 · Discovery & Research

I started by watching people fail to plan trips.

Before touching wireframes, I ran two weeks of discovery — 12 user interviews across three segments, plus competitive teardowns of 8 platforms.

⚡

9 of 12 users said they open multiple tabs and "settle" on a destination — they don't choose one confidently.

💬

Everyone described trips in adjectives — "relaxing," "adventurous," "offbeat." Nobody said "I want to go to Manali."

📊

Average 7+ tabs open before booking. Research-to-booking drop-off was the #1 conversion killer across all segments.

🏢

Corporate HR buyers needed zero-friction gifting — every existing tool forced destination selection upfront, killing the workflow.

03 · Design Process

Five weeks of structured iteration —
how I actually worked.

1

Define the IA

Three user types, one coherent system. Mapped the full IA across consumer, partner, and corporate surfaces to find shared components.

2

Low-fi explorations

14 wireframe concepts for search alone. Core tension: pure conversational AI vs structured filter input. Tested both — found neither worked alone.

3

Usability testing

3 rounds, 5 users each. Tested vibe search, itinerary output, and booking flow. Key finding: users needed a "confidence moment" before committing.

4

High-fi & handoff

Full component library in Figma with design tokens. 2-week engineering sprints. Wrote micro-copy, defined empty states, documented 40+ edge cases.

5

Post-launch iteration

Hotjar + analytics reviews bi-weekly. Two major package detail page iterations based on real drop-off data from month one.

Key Design Decisions
Challenge
❌ Earlier Direction
✓ Final Decision & Why
Search interaction model
Pure chat — "Talk to our AI"
Hybrid: vibe tags + free-text. Pure chat had 40% drop-off — users felt pressure to type "correctly."
Package result format
Dense spec list (nights, inclusions, price)
Visual card grid with vibe label first. Users decide on feel, then dive into details.
Itinerary depth in results
Full day-by-day itinerary shown upfront
Progressive disclosure: teaser → full on tap. Reduced overload, boosted click-throughs ~35%.
B2B vs Consumer navigation
Separate domain for corporate
Same domain, role-based surface switching. Shares trust signals, enables cross-sell.

Hidden gem

1. Vibe-First Search

Homepage leads with intent: "Tell us what you want to do." Hybrid of free text + vibe chips lowers the cognitive barrier. The AI surfaces destinations — not the user.

2. AI Chat Planning

Zuumm Chat lets users refine, adjust budgets, swap destinations, and build a full itinerary in one threaded conversation. No forms. No tab-switching.

3. Unified Platform

One design system, three surfaces. Consumer, partner, and corporate — same visual language, different permissions. Reduces training, increases trust.

Micro-UX Decisions
✍

Placeholder copy as a guide: Input reads "A week somewhere cold, just for me…" — teaching users how to speak the language before they type.

📄

Progressive disclosure: Day-wise itinerary hidden behind a soft toggle. Users commit with intent first, then explore the detail.

🔒

Trust signals at the right moment: IIM Bangalore badge and partner count surface at pre-booking — not crowding the hero.

🌐

Wander Notes as retention loop: Editorial repositioned from marketing to re-engagement — contextual links to bookable packages inline with content.

The most meaningful signal wasn't a number — it was a user quote: "It felt like talking to a travel-savvy friend, not filling out a form."

On the B2B side, corporate clients reported the new interface required zero onboarding for their employees — a first in their feedback history. The vibe-based input removed the need for training documentation entirely.

06 · Reflection

What I'd do differently —
and what I'm proud of.

🔁

I'd test the AI chat interface much earlier. We iterated on the vibe-tag system for weeks before finding users wanted a hybrid. Earlier guerrilla testing would've surfaced this in week one.

🏗

The IA needed a bolder multi-persona decision upfront. Early wireframes tried to force one navigation for all three user types. That cost 3 weeks of rework proper stakeholder alignment could have avoided.

✨

I'm most proud of the vibe-to-destination paradigm shift. Getting product, engineering, and business to trust a search experience that never asks "where do you want to go?" required conviction backed by user evidence.

🔭

What I'm watching next: Wander Notes has untapped potential as a full AI-driven discovery loop — generative travel stories linked directly to bookable packages. That's a product design story I'd love to build next.
